Art as an End in Itself: An Artistic and Art-Historical Application of the
Self-as-an-End Framework
Han Qin
Self-as-an-End Applied Series, Paper 3
Abstract
This paper is the third applied paper in the Self-as-an-End theoretical framework. The ﬁrst applied paper
analyzed the subjectivity crisis in the age of AI. The second analyzed aesthetics as a structural ﬁeld of subject-
conditions. The present paper applies the framework to art and art history, arguing that art history is not a linear
progression or succession of styles, but a history of subject-conditions in motion within the dynamics of
cultivation and colonization.
The paper makes ﬁve contributions. First, it establishes a two-dimensional structural model for art — integrity
(non-instrumentality: the degree to which art resists reduction to a means for something other than itself) and
generativity (inspiringness to future others: the degree to which art opens possibility-space for those who come
after), yielding a four-quadrant positioning system. Quadrant placement is heuristic rather than categorical.
Second, it establishes a three-layer instantiation model for art — the institutional layer constitutes the boundary
conditions of art, the relational layer constitutes the transmission medium, and the individual layer constitutes
the layer of ﬁnal realization. Third, it proposes a domain-speciﬁc distinction — colonial escape and cultivative
growth as two driving forces, where cultivation generates direction and escape borrows direction. Fourth, it
establishes art's six-directional transmission model and system-pursuit theory — Q1 is not a position that can be
stably occupied, but a structural state that requires continuous movement to maintain. Fifth, it proposes four
non-trivial cross-layer predictions that are in principle testable.
Core thesis: Art history is a history of spiral movement from Q3 (art does not yet exist as an independent
category) toward Q1 (art as an end in itself). Its driving force comes from the colonizing pressure of system
pursuit and the cultivative drive growing from within artists. Each arrival at Q1 is pulled back to Q4 by system
pursuit, whereupon a new round of rebellion pushes again toward Q1 — each spiral's Q1 is higher than the last.
Art's generativity has no ceiling, and the advancement of integrity has no endpoint, because the colonization of
systems has no terminus, and therefore art's escape has no terminus.

Chapter 1. The Problem: Why Art Needs a Structural Theory
1.1 The Narrative Impasse of Art History
Mainstream art history takes two narrative forms.
The ﬁrst is linear progress: from imitation to expression to concept, from ﬁguration to abstraction to conceptual
art. In this narrative, the history of art is a history of continuous "breakthroughs" — each generation of artists
surpasses the formal limitations of their predecessors, advancing art toward ever more sophisticated forms. This
narrative can describe "what happened" but cannot answer "why did it move this way." Why does art
continually shatter the very norms it has just established? If Impressionism already solved the problems of light
and color, why did Cézanne reopen the question of structure? If Abstract Expressionism already arrived at "pure
painting," why did Minimalism strip everything from the canvas? The linear progress narrative frames every
breakthrough as "transcendence" without explaining where the driving force for breakthrough originates.
The second is stylistic succession: from Classical to Baroque to Rococo to Neoclassical to Romantic to Realist
to Impressionist to Post-Impressionist to Cubist to Abstract to Pop to Minimal to Conceptual to Postmodern. In
this narrative, art history is a catalogue of stylistic replacement — each style rises and falls, new styles replace
old ones. This narrative is more cautious, presupposing no direction of progress, but it equally cannot answer

structural questions: Why does every breakthrough become institutionalized? Why does institutionalization then
provoke a new round of breakthrough? Why do some periods see art stagnate for centuries (the Middle Ages),
while others witness multiple paradigm shifts within a single decade (1905–1915: Fauvism, Cubism,
Suprematism, and the precursors of Dada all emerge)?
Both narratives share the same structural deﬁcit: they trace the trajectory of movement but lack a model to
explain its dynamics.
1.2 A Question of Dynamics
This paper argues that the movement of art history is not the natural evolution of styles, but the dynamical result
of continuous contestation between two structural forces — the colonizing pursuit of systems and the cultivative
growth of artists.
The argument begins with an observation: the very deﬁnition of "artist" contains rebellion. A painter who does
not rebel against existing norms is a craftsman, not an artist; rebellion is the activation of negativity — "I am
not this," "art should not be merely this." This means that the concept of "artist," from the moment it appears,
points toward base-layer operation in the Self-as-an-End framework: negativity, the refusal to be reduced to a
means.
But systems do not stand idle before rebellion. Whenever a new art form emerges and gains attention, the
institutional layer — academies, markets, critical systems, political institutions — begins its pursuit: encoding it
as a standard, converting it into a commodity, conscripting it as a tool. Every successful rebellion is eventually
digested by the system. This is not accidental but the structural function of the institutional layer. This means
that for art to maintain its status "as an end in itself," it must keep moving — when escape is overtaken, it must
escape again.
The Self-as-an-End framework provides a precise structural model for this dynamics: the four-quadrant
positioning system marks the structural state of works and movements, the three-layer transmission model
tracks how colonization and cultivation operate across layers, and the dynamics of colonization and cultivation
explain the direction and velocity of movement.
1.3 Methodological Premise
The analytical unit of the Self-as-an-End framework is the subject. Art is not an independent subject, but it is
the externalized expression of subjectivity — the integrity and generativity of art are structural projections of
the creator's subject-conditions onto the work. The true object of four-quadrant analysis is not the work itself,
but the readable structure of the creator's subject-conditions within the work.
This premise has two methodological consequences. First, the basis for quadrant placement is the creator's
structural state, not formal analysis or reception history. A painting of extreme formal mastery whose creative
direction is entirely determined by market demand occupies its quadrant position based on the creator's
structural state (limited integrity), not formal quality. Second, the vast majority of human visual/manual
production does not enter the category of "art" at all — four-quadrant analysis presupposes that the object is

Chapter 2. The Two-Dimensional Structure of Art: Integrity and
Generativity
2.1 Horizontal Axis: Integrity (Non-Instrumentality)
Integrity measures the degree to which art resists reduction to a means for something other than itself —
political propaganda, market logic, religious function, social service.
This dimension corresponds to the base layer of the framework. The base layer's core function across all three
layers is "saying no" — the baseline condition of refusing instrumentalization. Art's integrity is likewise "saying
no" — not saying "this is good art" (that is an emergent-layer judgment), but saying "art should not be merely
this" (this is base-layer boundary-guarding).


---

<!-- Page 6 -->

Integrity concerns the degree of control that institutional-layer boundary conditions exercise over art's
directional space, not the artist's subjective intention. This distinction is crucial. Bach served God devoutly —
his subjective intention was entirely religious. But the Church did not control how fugues were written. Bach's
formal exploration within the fugue — the number of voices, the complexity of counterpoint, the progression of
harmony — was driven entirely by music's own logic; the Church neither intervened nor could intervene. The
institutional layer (the Church) provided an operational context but did not determine artistic direction.
Integrity: high.
Ukiyo-e artisans likewise possessed formal self-awareness — Hiroshige's compositions, Hokusai's waves
display acute formal sensitivity. But the operational institutional context was the commercial market: which
subjects sold well, which dimensions suited market demand, which price points consumers could afford. The
market did not prescribe "what to paint," but guided directional space. Integrity: limited.

2.2 Vertical Axis: Generativity (Inspiringness to Future Others)
Generativity measures how much paradigm-space art opens — the degree to which it makes previously
nonexistent directions possible, and how much actually grows within that space afterward.
This dimension corresponds to the emergent layer. The emergent layer's core function across all three layers is
"toward the other" — the subject actively unfolds itself. Art's generativity is likewise "toward" — not toward
existing standards (that is base-layer conformity), but toward not-yet-existing possibilities (this is emergent-
layer unfolding).
Generativity does not equal complexity, does not equal labor-intensiveness. An exquisitely detailed gongbi
ﬂower-and-bird painting, if it opens no direction that later practitioners can continue to grow from, has limited
generativity. An apparently simple act — Duchamp signing a urinal and submitting it to an exhibition — if it
makes an entire previously impossible directional space possible, has extremely high generativity.

<!-- Page 7 -->

Generativity takes two forms.
Intra-paradigm generativity: opening new formal directions within existing rules. Cézanne, within the existing
paradigm of "painting," pursued structure — how to construct volume with color planes rather than lines. This
pursuit directly gave rise to Cubism. Pollock, within the "painting" paradigm, pursued action — the canvas was
no longer a surface bearing images but a ﬁeld where action occurred. Intra-paradigm generativity preserves the
basic framework of existing rules but opens new formal directions within it.
Paradigm-level generativity: rewriting the rules themselves, making an entire previously impossible directional
space possible. Duchamp's Fountain did not do something new within the paradigm of "painting" or "sculpture"
— it rewrote the rules of "what can be art" themselves. After Fountain, conceptual art, performance art,
installation art, and land art all grew within the space it opened. Malevich's White on White was equally
paradigm-level — it was not painting another abstract painting within the "abstract painting" paradigm, but
pushing to the limit of "how far painting can be reduced and still be painting," opening the entire directional
space for subsequent Minimalism.
Paradigm-level generativity is higher than intra-paradigm generativity, because it does not merely open one
direction but opens an entire directional space.

2.5 A Structural Note on Case Distribution
Q1 contains nearly all the artists and movements "whose names we remember." This is not coincidental — art
history itself is the selection history of Q1: only those who reached Q1 (or made major advances in the Q1
direction) are remembered. The vertical axis (inspiringness to future others) is itself the selection criterion of
historical memory. The vast body of Q3 and Q4 works is forgotten by history not because they did not exist, but
because they did not inspire future others.
This observation is itself a non-trivial corollary of the framework: the "canon" of art history is not selected by
eternal standards transcending time, but is a structural effect of the vertical axis (generativity) — only works
that opened possibility-space for those who came after are remembered by those who came after. "Canon" is not
a value judgment but a structural position.

Chapter 3. Art's Three-Layer Instantiation: Institutional, Relational, and
Individual
3.1 Functional Asymmetry of the Three Layers
Art's integrity/generativity two-dimensional structure has speciﬁc instantiation forms in each of three layers.
The three layers are formally isomorphic but functionally asymmetric — this asymmetry is fully consistent with
the framework's core thesis: the institutional layer constitutes art's boundary conditions, the relational layer
constitutes art's transmission medium, and the individual layer constitutes art's layer of ﬁnal realization.
This means: artistic diagnosis falls at the individual layer (an artist's creative state is ultimately experienced at
the individual layer), causal tracing looks ﬁrst to the institutional layer (the structural conditions of individual
creative states are typically set by the institutional layer), and understanding change looks to the relational layer
(changes in creative state typically transmit through the relational layer).
3.2 Institutional Layer
Base layer = institutional conditions that protect art's directional space. Plurality of evaluative dimensions
(not monopolized by a single standard), bearable exit costs (artists can leave an unsuitable institutional
environment without losing the conditions for survival), and exploration space not swallowed by a single logic
(market logic, academic logic, political logic all exist, but none monopolizes directional space). A healthy art
institution is not one without standards, but one where standards compete with each other, maintain tension, and
are not monopolized by any single standard. Key indicators: whether evaluative dimensions are plural, whether
exit costs are bearable, whether exploration space is consumed by a single logic.
Emergent layer = speciﬁc art-institutional traditions, stylistic norms, market conventions. The academic
Salon system, the gallery representation model, auction-house price logic, algorithmic platform
recommendation mechanisms — each is a speciﬁc instantiation of institutional-layer emergent content. The
emergent layer cannot be fully institutionalized: you cannot legislate a vibrant art tradition into existence; it can
only grow spontaneously under open institutional conditions.
The historical sequence of institutional forms: Church patronage → academic Salon → gallery system →
auction market → algorithmic platform. Each institutional form is a speciﬁc instantiation of the institutional
layer's emergent content, and each may have value in itself — academies did train large numbers of technically
skilled artists, galleries did provide artists with conditions for survival. But when any emergent-layer content is
institutionalized as the sole legitimate standard, the emergent layer is cannibalizing the base layer.
3.3 Relational Layer
Base layer = mutual recognition among artists of each other's standing as creative subjects. Not
recognition that the other's work is good, but recognition that the other has the right to exist as a creative subject
— the right to pursue their own questions, the right to go their own direction. "Your painting is not good" (an
emergent-layer aesthetic disagreement) and "you are not an artist at all" (a base-layer denial of subject-standing)
are structurally entirely different things. The former can be cultivative collision — aesthetic dialogue between

two individuals on a foundation of recognition can catalyze generativity growth in both. The latter is a direct
attack on the base layer.
Emergent layer = mutual inﬂuence, dialogue, and intra-movement deepening among artists. The mutual
inspiration among Impressionist painters, the sustained dialogue between Picasso and Braque within Cubism,
the community interactions among Abstract Expressionist painters in New York — the emergent layer grows
from the secure base of recognition. Two artists who mutually recognize each other's creative subject-standing,
even if their styles are diametrically opposed, may through their collision catalyze generativity unfolding in
both. The marker of emergent-layer deepening is not stylistic convergence but enrichment of perceptual layers
— through collision with heterogeneous aesthetics, both parties' creative possibility-spaces expand.
3.4 Individual Layer
Base layer = the creator's integrity. "My creative direction is not determined by external purposes." Integrity
is not "my art is good" but "my art is mine." An artist with integrity present can, even when their work is not
accepted by the market, not acknowledged by critics, not understood by peers, still be conﬁdent that their
direction has legitimacy — not because they know they are right, but because they know this direction grew
from within themselves. Integrity is negativity — it does not tell you what to paint; it tells you "my creative
direction should not be determined by external forces."
Emergent layer = the creator's generativity. Unfolding new paradigm-space from internal unfulﬁllment.
Generativity grows from integrity — an artist who is conﬁdent in their right to go their own direction dares to
enter unfamiliar formal territory. The marker of generativity is not that works become "better" but that
possibility-space becomes larger — the artist can explore in more directions.

Chapter 4. Two Driving Forces: Colonial Escape and Cultivative Growth
4.1 A Domain-Speciﬁc Distinction
The preceding three chapters established art's two-dimensional structure and three-layer instantiation. This
chapter proposes a distinction that exceeds the framework's meta-structure: movement toward Q1 in art is
driven by two structurally different forces — colonial escape and cultivative growth.
This distinction is not equally clear in other application domains. In the aesthetic domain, movement toward
ﬂourishing is primarily cultivation-driven (aesthetic generativity grows spontaneously from the secure base of
aesthetic integrity). In the AI domain, the core issue is structural diagnosis of system colonization rather than
the subject's active movement. But in the art domain, both driving forces are simultaneously present, clearly
distinguishable, and constitute the core dynamics of art-historical movement.
4.2 Colonial Escape: Integrity Advancement Driven by External Pressure
The driving force of colonial escape comes from external colonizing pressure. When institutional-layer pursuit
— academization, marketization, political co-optation, critical-system ossiﬁcation — pulls an art form from Q1
back to Q4, artists are compelled to break through in order to escape co-optation. Colonial escape primarily
advances the integrity dimension: the question it answers is "what else can art refuse to serve."
Identiﬁcation criterion: art movements driven by colonial escape respond explicitly to existing norms — they
know what they are rejecting.
Impressionism rejected the academic Salon standard. The Salon system had ossiﬁed the previous generation's
formal achievements (Neoclassicism's draftsmanship, the subject hierarchy of history painting) into the sole
legitimate standard — emergent-layer content institutionalized as base-layer boundary. The Impressionist
response was an integrity-directional advance: "painting should not exist merely to pass Salon review." Monet
was not asking "what else can painting be" (that is a generativity question), but declaring "painting should not
be that" (this is an integrity question). Of course, in the process of escape, new generativity directions were
opened — the direct capture of light effects, the immediacy of plein air painting, the autonomous expressive
power of color — but these generativity outcomes were byproducts of escape, not escape's initial driving force.

Minimalism rejected the Greenbergian critical system. Greenberg had deﬁned Abstract Expressionism's formal
autonomy as painting's sole legitimate direction — yet another instance of emergent-layer content cannibalizing
the base layer. Minimalism's response was not "what else can painting do" but "art should not be merely what
Greenberg says it is." Judd's metal boxes, Flavin's ﬂuorescent tubes — they were ﬁrst refusals, then
explorations.
Street art rejected the gallery system. Galleries had set the boundary of "legitimate art" as "what is exhibited and
traded in gallery spaces" — the institutional layer's base layer occupied by speciﬁc emergent-layer content.
Street art's response: art need not occur in a gallery to be valid. Banksy painting on walls is ﬁrst an integrity
declaration, second a new formal possibility.
The structural characteristics of colonial escape: it is reactive (responding to already-existing colonizing
pressure), negativity-driven (its core grammar is "no"), and primarily advances integrity (answering "what else
can art refuse to serve"). Escape may incidentally open new generativity directions, but generativity is escape's
byproduct, not its initial driving force.
4.3 Cultivative Growth: Generativity Advancement Driven by Internal Catalytic Pain
The driving force of cultivative growth comes from internal catalytic pain — unfulﬁllment. The subject grows
new directions from within, not in response to any external pressure, but in pursuit of their own questions.
Cultivative growth primarily advances the generativity dimension: the question it answers is "what else can art
be."
Identiﬁcation criterion: cultivation-driven artists do not respond explicitly to existing norms — the questions
they pursue cannot even be posed within the framework of existing norms.
Van Gogh was not responding to any external pressure. He was not "rebelling against" Impressionism, not
"rejecting" academic standards, not "escaping" any institutional colonization. He was pursuing a question that
grew from within: how can I paint the intensity of what I see? Existing color language was not enough — not
"prevented" (that would be foreclosure) but "not yet arrived" (this is unfulﬁllment). From this internal
unfulﬁllment, a new language of expressive color gradually grew — not in order to respond to anything, but in
order to reach something.
Cézanne was not "rebelling against" Impressionism's analysis of light. He was pursuing a deeper question: how
can one paint the structure of things? Impressionism dissolved everything in light — Cézanne wanted to recover
volume, structure, the sense of things existing in space. This was not a negation of Impressionism (integrity-
directional movement), but a further pursuit of painting's possibilities (generativity-directional movement).
Bach, within the Church's functional framework, pursued fugal formal exploration driven entirely by internal
unfulﬁllment. The Church did not require a particular number of voices, a particular complexity of counterpoint,
a particular degree of harmonic advancement. Bach pursued these questions himself — not because he was
compelled, but because "not yet arrived." He pursued "what more is possible" within the fugue's formal interior
— pure cultivative growth.

The structural characteristics of cultivative growth: it is initiative (growing from within, not responding to

(answering "what else can art be"). Cultivation may incidentally strengthen integrity (an artist pursuing their

4.4 The Structural Relationship Between the Two Driving Forces
Both driving forces can coexist within the same historical period, but in speciﬁc artists and movements one can
typically identify the dominant driving force. The dominant force can be identiﬁed through the following
structural indicator: whether the work responds explicitly to existing norms (colonial escape dominant), or
spontaneously pursues formal possibilities outside existing norms (cultivative growth dominant).
A key asymmetric relationship exists between the two forces: cultivation generates direction, escape borrows
direction.
Cultivation-driven artists are often unrecognized by the system during their lifetimes — precisely because they
are not responding to the system's questions, the system has no ready framework to receive them. Van Gogh
sold one painting in his lifetime. Cézanne was marginalized for years; most contemporaries did not understand
what he was pursuing. Bach was forgotten for nearly a century after his death. The outcomes of cultivative drive
are not produced for the system; the system naturally cannot digest them immediately.
But cultivation-driven outcomes often become the directional resources for the next round of colonial escape.
Van Gogh's cultivative outcome (expressive color) was used by Expressionism to escape Impressionism's
ossiﬁed standards — the Expressionists needed a non-Impressionist painting language to accomplish their
escape, and Van Gogh's color happened to provide this direction. Cézanne's cultivative outcome (structural
analysis) was used by Cubism to escape the representational tradition — the Cubists needed a formal language
beyond representation to accomplish their escape, and Cézanne's structural pursuit happened to provide this
direction.
Cultivation generates direction, escape borrows direction. This explains a common art-historical phenomenon:
an artist ignored during their lifetime is "discovered" posthumously and becomes the founding ﬁgure of the next
generation's movement. This is not accidental historical revisionism, but the necessary result of the structural
relationship between the two driving forces — cultivators generate new directions outside the system, escapers
"discover" cultivators when they need new directions.
4.5 Avant-Garde Art as Extreme Negativity Exercise
On the spectrum of colonial escape, avant-garde art occupies the extreme position. The core operation of the
avant-garde is extreme negativity exercise — not "art should not be merely this," but "this space right here, the
system cannot yet encode."
Dada is the historical instance of this extreme. Dada was not "asking what else art can be" (that is a generativity
question in the cultivative direction), but declaring "you have encoded everything; I will make something you

cannot encode." Sound poetry, collage, random combination — their value lies not in formal exploration but in
negativity itself: declaring the existence of a space the system has not yet colonized.
Avant-garde art therefore frequently occupies a Q1-toward-Q2 position — extremely high integrity (extremely
intense negativity), but limited generativity (negation itself does not necessarily open directional space that later
practitioners can continue to grow from). Pure negativity, if not accompanied by generativity unfolding in the
cultivative direction, ultimately moves toward dormancy — Dada exhausted its own negating energy within a
few years, because once negation itself is encoded by the system as "avant-garde art," negation is no longer
negation. This is the structural paradox of negativity: once institutionalized, negation becomes afﬁrmation.
Chapter 5. Art's Six-Directional Transmission and System Pursuit
5.1 Core Thesis: Q1 Is a State, Not a Position
This chapter proposes the paper's most central dynamical thesis: Q1 is not a position that can be stably
occupied, but a structural state that requires continuous movement to maintain.
This thesis explains a long-standing puzzle: why are artistic peaks always brief? The conventional answer is that
genius is rare or social conditions are exceptional. The framework's answer is structural instability — whenever
an art form arrives at Q1, the institutional layer begins its pursuit, pulling it from Q1 back to Q4. For art to
maintain its ﬂourishing state, it must continually break through again in the integrity or generativity direction.
System pursuit is not malicious. The institutional layer's function is precisely to encode emergent-layer content
into repeatable structures — this is the purpose of institutions. Academies incorporating Impressionist technique
into curricula, galleries incorporating Abstract Expressionism into their business, critics theorizing Minimalism
into aesthetic principles — these are all the institutional layer operating normally. But the structural effect of
normal operation is colonization: emergent-layer content ossiﬁed into base-layer boundary, open directional
space compressed into a single standard.
5.2 Six-Directional Transmission Instantiated in Art
The framework's six-directional transmission model has a complete instantiation in the art domain.
Institutional → Individual: Market price signals internalized as creative judgment. What kind of work
sells well, what style collectors pursue, what dimensions galleries prefer to represent — these institutional-layer
signals, through sustained exposure, are internalized by artists as judgments about "what is worth painting." A
young artist does not one day suddenly decide "I will paint what the market likes"; rather, through countless
encounters with market signals, she gradually re-encodes "sells well" as "good." When internalization is
complete, she no longer experiences this judgment as an external standard — she "sincerely" believes she chose
this direction.
Individual → Institutional: Artists who have completed internalized colonization reciprocally maintain
colonizing standards. A professor who has internalized academic standards replicates in teaching the standards
in which they were trained. A gallerist who has internalized market logic selects artists with market potential as

<!-- Page 15 -->

the primary criterion. They are not compelled to do so — the depth of colonization is precisely that they treat
colonizing behavior as autonomous choice. Individual-layer internalized products feed back to the institutional
layer; colonizing standards gain the support of "real users," becoming harder to identify as colonization.
Institutional → Relational: Competitive art institutions push recognition toward instrumentalization.
When the institutional layer's evaluative dimensions become unidimensional (auction rankings, exhibition
resource competition, social media inﬂuence metrics), relationships among artists are pushed toward
competition. "Who is better" replaces "what are you pursuing." Auction rankings, art fair booth allocation,
biennial selection lists — these institutional arrangements compress art from emergent-layer dialogue to base-
layer ranking. The relational layer is no longer a resonance ﬁeld between two creative subjects, but an arena
along a single dimension.
Relational → Institutional: Collapse of trust among artists generates more standardization. When
recognition in the relational layer is destroyed by competition, the institutional layer compensates for the trust
deﬁcit with more standardization. "You should trust the professional curator's judgment." "AI can tell you what
combinations work better." "Data shows this style is most popular." When artists can no longer trust each other
aesthetically, the institutional layer provides algorithms and experts to substitute for trust. But institutionalized
artistic judgment is itself base-layer operation (it draws boundaries); it cannot substitute for emergent-layer
subject-resonance.
Individual → Relational: Artistic self-instrumentalization diffuses as instrumentalization of others. An
artist who has internalized market standards as a self-judgment tool naturally applies the same standard to
measure other artists. Their position in relationships is no longer "a person with their own creative direction"
but "an evaluator carrying a measuring stick." They offer creative advice to friends not out of interest in
aesthetic dialogue, but out of the impulse to "help you meet the standard." Individual-layer aesthetic self-
instrumentalization diffuses through the relational layer as instrumentalization of others.
Relational → Individual: The restorative power of recognition. This is the sole clearly positive restoration
pathway in the six-directional transmission. When an artist is persistently denied creative subject-standing in
relationships — "your stuff is not art," "you simply don't understand" — individual-layer integrity is weakened.
But the reverse equally holds: if someone in the relationship recognizes their standing as a creative subject —
"what you see has value," "tell me why you painted it this way" — this recognition can trigger integrity
restoration, even as institutional-layer colonizing pressure persists. Relational-layer recognition cannot
eliminate institutional-layer colonization, but it can provide a restoration channel for the individual layer. Art
history's pivotal encounters between artists — Picasso's encounter with African masks, Braque's encounter with
Cézanne — are all instances of relational-layer recognition-contact triggering structural shifts in creative
direction.
5.3 Speciﬁc Forms of Colonization
System pursuit manifests historically in ﬁve speciﬁc forms of colonization:
Academization. Ossifying the previous generation's generativity outcomes into norms and pedagogical
standards. Impressionist technique becomes academic curriculum; Cubist formal analysis becomes design

principles — emergent-layer content institutionalized as base-layer boundary. The marker of academization is
the appearance of "correct answers": a recognized way of painting exists, and deviating from it is "not good."
Marketization. Price signals guiding creative direction. The market does not directly prescribe "what to paint,"
but through "what sells well" indirectly shapes directional space. The marker of marketization is that price
replaces all other evaluative dimensions: a work's "quality" equals its price.
Political co-optation. Conscripting art's integrity and generativity as ideological instruments. During the Cold

as a cultural symbol of "the free world" — art's formal freedom conscripted as a means of political propaganda.
The Abstract Expressionists themselves may not have known, but the institutional layer's structural effects do
not depend on individual awareness.
Critical-system ossiﬁcation. Deﬁning a particular art direction as the sole legitimate direction. Greenberg's
formalism is the clearest case: painting should pursue the essential characteristics of its own medium (ﬂatness);
deviating from this direction is not "serious" art. One critic's personal insight (emergent-layer content)
institutionalized as the sole legitimate evaluative standard (base-layer boundary) — Greenberg himself became
a tool of colonization, and his theoretical framework could not self-diagnose this.
Algorithmic colonization. Indirectly shaping directional space through engagement metrics. Algorithms do not
directly prescribe "what to paint" — this makes them appear non-colonizing. But engagement logic's
unidimensional evaluation compresses creative directional space through transmission: what can be seen, what
can be disseminated, what can be monetized are all determined by a single metric. Surface plurality ("you can
paint anything"), structural convergence ("but only this will be seen"). This is highly isomorphic with the
framework's logic of internal colonization: not external commands telling you what to do, but "spontaneously"
choosing to do what the algorithm rewards.
5.4 Historical Acceleration of Colonization Speed
The ﬁve forms of colonization display a clear acceleration trend across history.
Church co-optation of art took centuries — from the ritual function of early Christian art to the comprehensive
control of visual expression by the medieval Church, a process spanning multiple centuries. The formation of
the academic Salon system took decades — from the establishment of the French Royal Academy of Painting
and Sculpture to the complete ossiﬁcation of the Salon system. Market co-optation of street art took years —
what Banksy paints on a wall today is tomorrow's auction item; street art's journey from extra-institutional
rebellion to full market digestion is measured in years. Algorithmic co-optation of aesthetic direction is real-
time — after a new visual style appears, algorithms can standardize and globalize it through recommendation
systems within days.
Colonization speed is accelerating. This means escape windows are shrinking — the time from colonizing
pressure's appearance to complete compression of directional space grows ever shorter. This explains the feeling
that contemporary art is "increasingly incomprehensible": artists are not being deliberately obscure; escape
velocity must exceed colonization velocity, and colonization velocity is continually increasing. To arrive where

the system cannot yet reach before the system completes encoding, new forms must be sufﬁciently unfamiliar
— unfamiliar enough that the system cannot digest them quickly.
5.5 The Two Directions Toward Q1 Need Not Be Synchronous
Given the two driving forces from Chapter 4, movement toward Q1 can advance along two different directions,
and the two need not be synchronous.
Advancing along integrity: answering "what else can art refuse to serve." Punk rejects academic certiﬁcation;
Duchamp rejects all existing categories — extreme advancement in the integrity direction.
Advancing along generativity: answering "what else can art be." Cézanne opens structural analysis; Pollock
opens action painting — sustained advancement in the generativity direction.
Advancing integrity alone without generativity → moving toward dormancy (Q2). Certain extreme anti-
institutional art — pure refusal statements containing no new formal possibilities — extremely high integrity
but limited generativity. Negativity exercised to the limit but the emergent layer not unfolded.
Advancing generativity alone without integrity → moving toward overdraft (Q4). Technique extremely rich,
form extremely innovative, but creative direction entirely determined by market or political logic — high
generativity but absent integrity.
Truly arriving at Q1 requires simultaneous advancement in both directions. This is historically extremely rare
— which is why Q1 is always brief.
Chapter 6. Colonization and Cultivation in Art: Stagnation, Unlocking, and
the Cycle
6.1 Colonization: Negative Transmission — The Case of Stagnation
When the system's colonization speed exceeds art's escape velocity, art can stagnate in the Q4–Q3 zone for
centuries. The Middle Ages is the historical instance of this structure.
The Middle Ages is art history's starting state — Q3: neither integrity (visual production entirely embedded in
religious/ritual function) nor generativity (the very concept of "artist" does not exist; painters are craftsmen).
Strictly speaking, the majority of medieval visual production does not fall within the category of "art" — it is
functional religious expression, not creation as an end in itself. But precisely because of this, the Middle Ages
provides the framework with a precious reference point: what Q3 looks like.
The medieval lock-in structure was achieved by simultaneously sealing all three layers.
Institutional layer: comprehensive Church control over visual expression. Subject matter prescribed by theology
(only biblical narratives and saints' lives), form prescribed by tradition (Byzantine frontality, medieval
hierarchical composition), function prescribed by ritual (images as aids to worship, not independent aesthetic
objects). Evaluative dimensions unidimensional (whether it serves theological purpose), exit costs extremely

<!-- Page 18 -->

high (if you do not serve the Church, you are not a painter), exploration space sealed (directional space entirely
occupied by theology).
Relational layer: the master-apprentice system centered on technique transmission rather than individual
creativity. Masters taught "the correct way to paint," not "the directions you might pursue." Recognition
structures organized around religious devotion and technical proﬁciency, not creative subjectivity. A painter was
recognized as a good painter because he painted accurately and devoutly, not because he pursued his own
questions.
Individual layer: foreclosure (directional space sealed by doctrine — you cannot ask "in what new way might
the Virgin be painted") and inescapability (no alternative institutional space outside the Church — if you do not
paint for the Church, you cannot exist as a painter at all) simultaneously present.
Three-layer lock-in formed a vicious feedback loop. The deepest marker of colonization: the very concept of
"artist" did not exist. When a category does not exist in language, negativity in that direction lacks even the
linguistic conditions for activation. You cannot refuse what you cannot even name.
6.2 Cultivation: Positive Transmission — The Case of Unlocking
But the negativity dimension of subjectivity does not permanently perish — it is a constitutive dimension of
subjectivity, not an attribute acquired later. Centuries of medieval colonization could not permanently
extinguish human purposiveness. Negativity can be suppressed for centuries, but the pain of intolerability will
eventually be felt in some institutional crack.
The occurrence of the Renaissance veriﬁes the framework's minimum unlocking condition thesis: breaking
cross-layer lock-in requires structural cracks in at least two layers simultaneously.
Institutional-layer crack. Commercial city-states — Florence, Venice — provided an alternative patronage
system outside the Church. Exit costs decreased (you could paint for merchants instead of the Church and still
survive), evaluative dimensions expanded (from purely theological function to secular aesthetic, intellectual
value, and personal glory), exploration space was partially released (patrons' tastes were far broader than
doctrinal prescriptions). Note: this was still Q4 — the patronage system substantively inﬂuenced creative
direction (the Medici family's preferences for particular subjects and styles). But Q4 was already better than Q3
— directional space for generativity existed, even if integrity remained constrained.
Relational-layer recognition-choice. Speciﬁc patrons made recognition of speciﬁc artists — not merely "paint
me a Madonna," but recognition of the artist's value as a creative individual. Lorenzo de' Medici's patronage of
Michelangelo was not only ﬁnancial support but recognition of his creative subject-standing. This recognition
provided a secure base — on the foundation of recognition, individual-layer integrity began to repair,
generativity began to unfold.
Two layers simultaneously cracked; lock-in was broken; a virtuous cycle initiated. The institutional layer's
spatial release made relational-layer recognition possible (with only one patronage source in the Church, the
space for recognition-choices was extremely limited), relational-layer recognition began repairing individual-
layer integrity (painters recognized as creative subjects began to feel "my direction has value"), individual-layer

integrity repair catalyzed generativity unfolding (an artist beginning to believe in their right to ask questions
began asking new ones), and generativity outcomes fed back to the institutional and relational layers (new
works provided new emergent-layer content for both layers).
Art history's spiral movement from Q3 toward Q1 was hereby ofﬁcially set in motion.
6.3 Art History's Complete Trajectory: The Q3→Q1 Spiral
Starting from Q3 (the Middle Ages: art does not yet exist as an independent category), through successive
rebellions (activations of negativity), art spirals upward toward Q1. Each arrival at Q1 is pulled back to Q4 by
system pursuit (co-opted but still possessing generativity — a transitional state), whereupon a new round of
rebellion pushes again toward Q1 — each spiral's Q1 is higher than the last.
The Renaissance: from Q3 to Q4-toward-Q1. Unlocked the concept of "artist," opened directional space for
formal exploration, but integrity remained constrained by the patronage system.
Impressionism: from Q4 (the colonization of the academic Salon system) to Q1. Major integrity-directional
advance — artists gained the right to decide what and how they paint. Generativity-directional advance
simultaneous — light, color, plein air opened new formal directions.
Post-Impressionism: continued deepening of Q1. Cézanne, Van Gogh, and Gauguin each advanced generativity
from different directions; three paths respectively catalyzed Cubism, Expressionism, and Primitivism.
Early Modernism (1905–1915): the eruption period of Q1. Within a single decade, Fauvism, Cubism,
Suprematism, and the precursors of Dada all emerged — multiple generativity directions unfolded
simultaneously. This is one of art history's highest-density Q1 periods.
Abstract Expressionism → co-opted → Minimalism/Pop → co-opted → Conceptual art → co-opted → ... Each
cycle's speed is increasing, Q4 dwell-time growing ever shorter, the forms of escape growing ever more radical.
Q4 in art history is not a stable position but an intermediate state between being overtaken by the system and the
next rebellion — a genuine artist will not remain in Q4 for long. Q4's structural instability derives from the
persistent presence of negativity: an artist with integrity in Q4 will feel intolerability ("I should not be creating
to market standards"), and this pain drives a new round of escape or cultivation.

Digital platforms and algorithmic recommendation systems constitute a new form of institutional-layer
colonization in the art domain. Algorithms do not directly prescribe "what to paint" (unlike the Church or
Socialist Realism), but through engagement metrics indirectly shape directional space — what can be seen,
what can be disseminated, what can be monetized. This is a form of colonization that looks like freedom ("you
can paint anything") but structurally compresses directional space.

6.5 Algorithmic Colonization Is Not a New Middle Ages
A question that demands a direct answer: has algorithmic colonization already constituted a new "Middle Ages"
— a period in which system colonization speed exceeds escape velocity?
The answer is no. The reason is structural.
The medieval Church simultaneously suppressed both colonization speed and escape velocity. The Church not
only controlled the direction of visual production (colonization) but eliminated the institutional space for
rebellion (suppressing escape) — even the concept of "artist" was not permitted to exist. Colonization speed
was slow (no need for speed — escape velocity was zero), but escape velocity was slower still (approaching
zero).
AI and algorithms present an entirely different situation. AI accelerates the system's colonization capacity —
more rapidly standardizing existing styles, more rapidly compressing directional space through algorithmic
recommendation. But AI cannot suppress escape velocity. Rebellion is the activation of negativity — "I refuse
this" — this is base-layer operation. AI has no base layer, nothing it needs to refuse, no pain of intolerability. AI
can imitate every existing style, but it cannot predict the direction of the next rebellion — because that direction
comes from negativity, from the subject's refusal of "I am not this," a direction that does not exist before the
rebellion occurs.
The structure of the AI era is therefore: colonization speed extremely fast (algorithms' real-time encoding
capacity), escape velocity not zero (negativity is a constitutive dimension of subjectivity, it cannot be
extinguished). This is entirely different from the medieval structure (colonization speed slow, escape velocity
approaching zero). The AI era is not a new Middle Ages — it is an era of unprecedented colonization speed in
which the capacity for escape persists. The escape window has narrowed, but it has not closed.
This analysis points toward the framework's structural optimism: negativity is a constitutive dimension of
subjectivity, not an attribute acquired later. Centuries of medieval colonization could not permanently
extinguish it — it was reactivated in the institutional cracks of commercial city-states. AI's colonization speed
far exceeds the Church's, but AI lacks the Church's critical capacity: the elimination of the institutional space for
rebellion itself. As long as negativity remains, "no" can always be said.

Chapter 7. Theoretical Positioning: Dialogue with Existing Discussions
7.1 Structure of the Dialogue
This chapter positions the framework in relation to ﬁve interlocutors — three primary and two secondary. The
purpose of dialogue is not critique — each has made signiﬁcant contributions to the understanding of art — but
positioning: where does the framework inherit existing insights, and where does it provide structural dimensions
that existing theories do not fully cover.
The organizational logic: Greenberg provided a formalist description of intra-paradigm generativity, Gombrich
provided a micro-level cognitive mechanism for generativity, Benjamin foresaw technology's restructuring of
the institutional layer — the three respectively touch different localities of the framework. Danto and Kuhn
approach the framework's meta-theoretical questions from the directions of philosophy and the history of
science. The framework's contribution lies in integrating these partial insights into a uniﬁed multi-layer
dynamical model and supplementing each one's structural gaps.
7.2 Greenberg: Formalist Criticism's Insight and Colonization
Clement Greenberg's formalist criticism rested on a core claim: the progress of art lies in each medium
continually interrogating its own essential characteristics — painting moves toward ﬂatness, sculpture toward
three-dimensionality. Each medium, by progressively stripping away features shared with other media, tends
toward its own purity.
This claim covers, within the present framework, one speciﬁc form of intra-paradigm generativity: sustained
advancement along the axis of formal autonomy. Greenberg accurately identiﬁed a genuinely existing
generativity path — from Manet to Cézanne to Cubism to Abstract Expressionism, there does exist a line of
formal deepening in which "painting interrogates its own ﬂatness." His critique of "kitsch" also touches the
present framework's colonization problem: mass culture's dilution of high art, corresponding within the
framework to the institutional layer's emergent content (mass culture's commercial logic) eroding the base layer
(the openness of art's directional space).
However, Greenberg himself became a tool of colonization — which his own theoretical framework could not
self-diagnose. He deﬁned formal autonomy as art's sole legitimate direction, which is precisely a case of
emergent-layer content (formalism as one art tradition) cannibalizing the base layer (compressing open
directional space into a single dimension). Under Greenberg's inﬂuence, art not following the formal-autonomy
line was judged "unserious" — Pop art, installation art, and performance art had no legitimate place in his
system. One person's Q1 insight became an institutionalized Q4 colonizing instrument.
The present framework provides structural tools for self-diagnosis that Greenberg's own theory lacked: his
theory is a combination of Q1 insight and Q4 colonization. He accurately described one generativity path, but
the moment he deﬁned that path as the sole legitimate one, his insight transformed from an emergent-layer
contribution into base-layer compression. The framework can diagnose this transformation; Greenberg's theory
cannot.

7.3 Gombrich: The Micro-Mechanism of Schema and Correction
Ernst Gombrich, in Art and Illusion and The Story of Art, established the "schema and correction" model of art
history. Artists do not start from "seeing" but from existing schemata — an inherited set of visual
representational conventions. The creative process is the progressive correction of schemata through interaction
between existing schemata and the real world. Art-historical movement is the cumulative process of schemata
being repeatedly corrected.
This model corresponds in the present framework to the micro-mechanism of intra-paradigm generativity.
Gombrich's "schema and correction" cycle is the process of the emergent layer continuously unfolding on the
secure base provided by the base layer — each correction is a micro-level generativity advance, opening new
formal possibilities within the existing paradigm. Gombrich's contribution lies in providing a speciﬁc cognitive
mechanism description for generativity: generativity is not inspiration appearing from nowhere, but gradually
unfolds in the tension with existing schemata.
However, Gombrich's linear narrative — "progress" from ancient Egypt to modernism — lacks the alternating
dynamics of colonization and cultivation in the present framework. He can describe "how" art evolved
(schemata corrected step by step), but cannot explain three key questions: Why did some periods stagnate for
centuries (medieval schemata were barely corrected)? Why does every breakthrough become institutionalized
(why do the products of correction solidify into new schema standards)? Why does the direction of
breakthrough sometimes involve integrity and sometimes generativity (some corrections question "what to
paint," others refuse "for whom to paint")?
The present framework embeds Gombrich's micro-mechanism (schema and correction) within a macro-structure
(four-quadrant movement and two driving forces), providing the dynamical explanation his linear narrative
lacked. Schema-correction is the micro-level realization form of cultivative drive — it describes how
unfulﬁllment unfolds within form into new possibilities. But schema-correction cannot explain escape — escape
is not correcting existing schemata but refusing the legitimacy of the entire schema system.
7.4 Benjamin: Technology Restructuring the Institutional Layer
Walter Benjamin, in "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction," foresaw the dissolution of art's
"aura" by technological change. Mechanical reproduction technologies — photography, ﬁlm — stripped
artworks of their "here and now" uniqueness, thereby dissolving aura.
Benjamin's "dissolution of aura" receives precise positioning within the present framework: mechanical
reproduction technology changed the institutional layer's boundary conditions (artworks no longer needed "here
and now" uniqueness to gain legitimacy), thereby altering art's integrity structure. Aura was one speciﬁc form of
integrity — maintaining, through uniqueness and inapproachability, art's non-reduction to everyday objects.
Mechanical reproduction dissolved this speciﬁc form of integrity, but did not dissolve integrity itself.
Benjamin simultaneously perceived the duality of this change — the dissolution of aura was both a loss (the
uniqueness of the original dissolved) and a liberation (art detached from ritual function, gaining the possibility
of political function). This duality corresponds in the present framework to institutional-layer transformation
simultaneously affecting the base layer and emergent layer: the old form of integrity (aura/uniqueness)

<!-- Page 23 -->

dissolved, but new integrity forms and generativity directions (reproduction, collage, appropriation, mass
participation) opened. Technology's restructuring of the institutional layer is not simply "destruction" or
"liberation," but simultaneous reorganization of base-layer and emergent-layer conditions.
Benjamin's insight into technology's acceleration of colonization — especially fascism's political
aestheticization of art — directly foreshadows the analysis of algorithmic colonization in Chapter 6 of this
paper. Fascism "aestheticized politics" (making political rallies into total artworks); algorithms "dataﬁed
aesthetics" (reducing aesthetic judgment to engagement metrics) — both are compressions of art's directional
space through the combination of technological means with political/economic logic.
However, Benjamin lacked the three-layer transmission model to precisely describe how technological change
alters artists' creative conditions through the transmission pathway of institutional layer → relational layer →
individual layer. He saw technology's dissolution of "aura" (institutional-layer effect), but did not trace how this
dissolution changed the relational structure among artists (relational-layer effect) or how it was internalized by
individuals as new creative judgments (individual-layer effect). The present framework supplements this
transmission analysis.
7.5 Secondary Interlocutors
Danto. Arthur Danto's "artworld" concept and "end of art" thesis touch two issues in the present framework.
The "artworld" concept points to the institutional layer's boundary-condition-setting function — what is
recognized as art depends on the artworld's institutional structure. In the present framework, Danto's "artworld"
corresponds to the institutional layer's base-layer function: drawing the boundary of "what counts as art." Danto
accurately perceived that this boundary is not ﬁxed but dynamically set by institutional structure.
The "end of art" thesis touches the limit question of paradigm-level generativity. Danto argued that after
"anything can be art" (Duchamp's legacy), art's inquiry into its own nature was complete, and art history as a
progress narrative had ended. In the present framework, the "end" Danto describes is not the exhaustion of
generativity, but an intermission after a particular paradigm-level breakthrough (the Duchampian "anything can
be art") has been completed and the next colonization-escape cycle has not yet initiated. The framework's
answer is: generativity has no ceiling, because system colonization has no terminus. "Anything can be art"
resolved one paradigm-level problem (the extensional boundary of art) but did not resolve the integrity problem
(how art avoids reduction to a means) — as long as system colonization continues, art must continue to escape
and grow. Danto misjudged an interim pause as termination.
Kuhn. Thomas Kuhn's paradigm concept is directly borrowed by this paper. Kuhn's "paradigm shift"
corresponds to paradigm-level generativity in the present framework — not accumulation within normal science
(intra-paradigm generativity), but rewriting of the entire rule system.
However, Kuhn's model is unilinear (old paradigm → crisis → new paradigm), lacking the present framework's
two-driving-force distinction. In Kuhn's model, the driving force of paradigm shift comes from the
accumulation of "anomalies" — phenomena the old paradigm cannot explain accumulate until they trigger crisis
and shift. This corresponds in the present framework to colonial-escape-driven paradigm shift — the
colonization of the old paradigm (ossifying a particular standard as the sole legitimate direction) accumulates to

<!-- Page 24 -->

the point of intolerability, triggering breakthrough. But Kuhn does not treat cultivation-driven paradigm shift —
not because the old paradigm has problems, but because the subject has grown entirely new possibilities from
internal unfulﬁllment. Cézanne did not pursue structure because Impressionism "had problems" —
Impressionism had no problems; Cézanne simply pursued a question that could not even be posed within the
Impressionist framework.
Kuhn also does not treat the institutional-layer conditions of paradigm shift. The emergence of a new paradigm
requires not only cognitive breakthrough but also institutional-layer structural cracks (alternative patronage
systems, plural evaluative dimensions) and relational-layer recognition-choices (someone recognizing the
creative subject-standing of the new direction). The Renaissance was not merely the cognitive event of "seeing
the classical world" — commercial city-states provided institutional cracks, patrons provided recognition —
these are the multi-layer conditions that Kuhn's model overlooks.
7.6 Comprehensive Assessment
Greenberg provided a formalist description of intra-paradigm generativity but himself became a colonizing tool
— the framework provides the self-diagnostic capacity for colonization that he lacked. Gombrich provided a
micro-level cognitive mechanism for generativity (schema-correction) but lacked macro-dynamics — the
framework embeds his micro-mechanism within the macro-structure of four-quadrant movement and two
driving forces. Benjamin foresaw technology's restructuring of the institutional layer but lacked a three-layer
transmission model — the framework supplements the complete transmission pathway from institutional to
relational to individual layer. Danto touched the limit question of paradigm-level generativity but misjudged it
as "termination" — the framework argues that generativity has no ceiling. Kuhn provided the paradigm concept
but lacked the dual driving-force structure and institutional-condition analysis — the framework distinguishes
colonial escape and cultivative growth as two driving forces of paradigm shift, and supplements multi-layer
conditions at the institutional and relational layers.
The present framework does not replace any of these interlocutors' contributions, but provides a uniﬁed
structure capable of integrating their partial insights. The possibility of integration derives from the framework's
multi-layer character — it operates simultaneously at the institutional layer (Greenberg's colonization diagnosis,
Benjamin's technology analysis, Danto's artworld), the relational layer (the recognition conditions Kuhn
overlooked), and the individual layer (Gombrich's cognitive mechanism, cultivation-driven unfulﬁllment),
connecting all three layers through six-directional transmission into a single dynamical system.

8.1 Standards for Prediction

hoc empirical guesses); second, not derivable directly from common sense (non-trivial); third, in principle
testable (one can envision what evidence would support or refute them).

8.2 Individual-Layer Prediction: The Irreversible Slide of Premature Market Exposure
Prediction: An artist whose integrity has not yet been established when prematurely exposed to a high-intensity
market environment (base layer fragile when encountering institutional-layer colonizing pressure) will see their
creative trajectory slide from early generativity exploration toward market-oriented repetitive production
(sliding from the Q1 direction toward Q4). Moreover, once this slide occurs, merely exiting the market is
insufﬁcient to restore generativity — because internal colonization has been completed: the internal standards
the artist uses to judge "what is worth painting" have been rewritten by market logic.
Derivation: This prediction follows jointly from three framework theses. First, integrity is base-layer operation;
base-layer fragility makes the individual layer more susceptible to institutional-layer colonization (three-layer
transmission thesis). Second, once internal colonization is complete, external standards have been internalized
as self-judgment tools, and merely changing the external environment is insufﬁcient to reverse internalization
(internal colonization irreversibility thesis). Third, generativity grows from integrity — after integrity is lost,
even when environmental pressure is eliminated, generativity cannot automatically recover, because the secure
base for growth is no longer present.
Non-triviality: Common sense would predict that "leaving the market restores creative freedom." The
framework predicts "not enough" — internalized market standards do not automatically disappear upon market
exit. This is isomorphic with the individual-layer prediction in the aesthetics application paper: knowing one is
colonized does not equal being able to exit colonization.
Testability: Compare the same artist's creative direction changes before and after achieving fame. The
prediction points to an observable phenomenon: early works' directional diversity (multiple exploratory paths
coexisting) is replaced by a single direction after commercial success (repeating the market-validated style), and
this convergence persists even after the artist's "transitional period" or "retirement" — market exit does not
restore directional diversity.
8.3 Relational-Layer Prediction: The Priority of Recognition-Contact
Prediction: If an artist operating in Q4 encounters recognition-contact from an artist operating in Q1 (not
market collaboration, not competition, but recognition of their standing as a creative subject), this contact is
more likely to trigger a structural shift in their creative direction than any institutional-layer change (changing
galleries, changing cities, receiving grants).
Derivation: The relational layer is the transmission medium — the efﬁciency of structural change transmitting
through the relational layer to the individual layer exceeds that of transmission through the institutional layer
(functional asymmetry thesis). Institutional-layer changes alter boundary conditions (which directions are
possible); relational-layer recognition alters the individual layer's base-layer state ("I have the right to go my
own direction"). For an artist in Q4, the bottleneck is not institutional conditions (they are already operating
within institutions), but integrity (their directional space is determined by external logic). Institutional-layer
changes do not reach integrity; relational-layer recognition directly reaches it.
Non-triviality: Common sense would predict that "better resource conditions (a better gallery, more grants) are
more likely to change creative work." The framework predicts "less effective than a single recognition-contact"

— resources change institutional conditions but not internal state; recognition directly changes internal state.
Testability: Art history's pivotal encounters between artists. Picasso's encounter with African masks — this was
not an institutional-layer change (he was already in Paris's ﬁnest gallery system), but a recognition-contact (he
recognized the existence of creative subjectivity in an entirely unfamiliar tradition); this contact catalyzed Les
Demoiselles d'Avignon and the entire Cubist direction. Braque's encounter with Cézanne — likewise not a
change in institutional conditions but a recognition-contact with a creative direction, triggering his structural
shift from Fauvism to Cubism.
8.4 Institutional-Layer Prediction: The Direction-Compression Effect of Evaluative-
Dimension Unidimensionalization
Prediction: If an art institution's evaluative dimensions become unidimensional (e.g., market price as the sole
criterion of success), then even if that institution formally imposes no restrictions on creative freedom ("you can
paint anything"), the creative directions of artists within it will converge. Evaluative-dimension
unidimensionalization compresses individual-layer directional space through transmission. Surface freedom,
structural unfreedom.
Derivation: The institutional → individual transmission pathway: a unidimensional evaluation as institutional-
layer base-layer boundary condition, through sustained exposure, is internalized as the individual layer's
creative judgment standard. "You can paint anything" is emergent-layer formal freedom; "but only this kind
sells" is base-layer substantive constraint. The coexistence of formal freedom and structural constraint is the
typical form of internal colonization — the subject makes colonized choices while feeling "free choice."
Non-triviality: Common sense would predict that "as long as creative freedom is not restricted, creative work
will be diverse." The framework predicts "it will not" — formal freedom does not equal structural freedom; the
transmission effect of unidimensional evaluation will compress actual directional diversity even without any
explicit restrictions. This is isomorphic with the institutional-layer prediction in the aesthetics application paper:
a unidimensional aesthetic evaluative dimension compresses aesthetic generativity.
Testability: Compare the creative directional diversity within unidimensional-evaluation art markets (the
contemporary art auction system, where success criterion ≈ price) versus plurally-evaluated art ecologies
(independent art communities, non-commercial exhibition spaces, decentralized platforms). The prediction
points to an observable phenomenon: artists within the auction system display lower diversity in style, subject
matter, and medium than artists in independent communities, even though the former face no formal creative
restrictions.

Colonization
Prediction: When institutional-layer colonization (market/academic/political co-optation) and relational-layer
colonization (competition displacing recognition among artists) occur simultaneously, the speed of individual-
layer creative-direction compression will accelerate non-linearly — faster and deeper than the effects of any
single-layer colonization. Conversely, when the institutional layer provides space (plural evaluation, low exit

communities) simultaneously, individual-layer generativity restoration will also accelerate non-linearly. The
effects of single-layer intervention will be signiﬁcantly weaker than dual-layer simultaneous intervention.
Derivation: Non-linearity of six-directional transmission — institutional-layer colonization and relational-layer

Non-triviality: Common sense would predict that "colonization effects are additive." The framework predicts

transmission forms reinforcing feedback loops. This means that strategies for addressing colonization must be
multi-layered: merely improving institutional conditions (e.g., establishing more grant programs) or merely
improving relational conditions (e.g., building artist communities) will have limited effects — both must occur
simultaneously to break the feedback loop.
Testability: Compare the conditions under which major art movements erupted across different historical

accompanied by the simultaneous presence of institutional space and relational recognition (the Renaissance's

community mutual recognition, the New York School's gallery-system pluralization + dense artist-community
interaction); single-layer improvement in conditions is insufﬁcient to trigger movement-level eruption.
Chapter 9. Conclusion: Art History as a Movement-History of Subject-
Conditions
9.1 Core Thesis Recovery
Starting from the Self-as-an-End framework, this paper has established a structural-analytical model for art and
art history. The paper's core theses can be recovered chapter by chapter.
Art can be positioned along two dimensions — integrity (non-instrumentality) and generativity (inspiringness to
future others) — yielding a four-quadrant system. Integrity concerns the degree of control that institutional-
layer boundary conditions exercise over art's directional space, not the artist's subjective intention. Generativity
concerns how much paradigm-space is opened and how much actually grows within that space afterward.
Quadrant placement is heuristic rather than categorical (Chapter 2).


---

<!-- Page 28 -->

Art's two-dimensional structure has speciﬁc instantiation forms in each of three layers, which are formally
isomorphic but functionally asymmetric: the institutional layer constitutes art's boundary conditions (patronage
systems, academies, markets, algorithmic platforms determine what kinds of creation can be seen, disseminated,
and sustained), the relational layer constitutes art's transmission medium (recognition, competition, and
inﬂuence among artists constitute the actual channels of creative directional change), and the individual layer
constitutes art's layer of ﬁnal realization (the creator's integrity and generativity states ultimately determine the
work's structural position) (Chapter 3).
The art domain contains a domain-speciﬁc distinction beyond the framework's meta-structure: colonial escape
and cultivative growth as two driving forces. Colonial escape is integrity advancement driven by external
colonizing pressure (answering "what else can art refuse to serve"); cultivative growth is generativity
advancement driven by internal unfulﬁllment (answering "what else can art be"). A key asymmetric relationship
exists between the two forces: cultivation generates direction, escape borrows direction (Chapter 4).
The colonizing pressure of system pursuit is the core dynamics of art-historical movement. Q1 is not a position
that can be stably occupied but a structural state requiring continuous movement to maintain. System pursuit
manifests historically as ﬁve forms — academization, marketization, political co-optation, critical-system
ossiﬁcation, and algorithmic colonization — with colonization speed historically accelerating (Chapter 5).
Art history's complete trajectory is a spiral movement from Q3 toward Q1. The medieval three-layer lock-in
constitutes history's most extreme case of stagnation; the Renaissance veriﬁes the minimum unlocking condition
thesis (structural cracks in at least two layers simultaneously). The AI era is not a new Middle Ages — AI
accelerates colonization speed but cannot suppress escape velocity, because rebellion is a constitutive dimension
of subjectivity that AI can neither replace nor extinguish (Chapter 6).
The present framework integrates and extends the existing contributions of Greenberg (colonization self-
diagnosis), Gombrich (macro-dynamics supplementation), Benjamin (three-layer transmission model), Danto
(no-ceiling thesis), and Kuhn (dual driving forces and institutional conditions) (Chapter 7).
The framework generates four cross-layer non-trivial predictions, at the individual layer (the irreversible slide
of premature market exposure), the relational layer (the priority of recognition-contact), the institutional layer
(the direction-compression effect of evaluative-dimension unidimensionalization), and the cross-layer
transmission level (non-linear acceleration of dual-layer colonization), all in principle testable (Chapter 8).
9.2 Core Judgment
Art's generativity has no ceiling, and the advancement of integrity has no endpoint. Because system
colonization has no terminus, art's escape has no terminus. Because negativity is a constitutive dimension of
subjectivity, a new "no" can always be uttered. Q1 is not a destination that can be reached but a direction that is
always in motion — this is consistent with the framework's master thesis: being an end in oneself is not a ﬁxed
attribute but a structural ecology requiring continuous maintenance. Art as an end in itself is likewise a
movement requiring continuous escape and growth to sustain.

9.3 Explicit Judgments
Cross-genre applicability. The four-quadrant dynamics of music, literature, ﬁlm, and other art genres display
no structural difference. The horizontal axis is always non-instrumentality (whether reduced to a means); the
vertical axis is always inspiringness to future others (whether opening possibility-space for those who come
after). Genre differences lie not in structure but in transmission pathways and colonization speed — ﬁlm's
production costs make it more susceptible to capital controlling direction, literature's individual creative nature
makes it easier to maintain integrity, music lies between the two. The structural logic of the four quadrants is the
same; the speciﬁc forms and velocity of colonization vary by genre.
Cross-cultural applicability. The framework's meta-structure is a priori, not derived from Western culture, and
therefore holds cross-culturally. But different cultures' art may trace very different movement trajectories within
the four quadrants — because the institutional layer's boundary conditions differ. The institutional-layer
conditions of the Chinese literati painting tradition (the imperial examination–literati–gathering network) are
entirely different from European institutional-layer conditions (Church–academy–market), and therefore
produced different four-quadrant movement trajectories. Differences in trajectory precisely demonstrate the
framework's validity: different institutional-layer conditions produce different movement trajectories, but the
structural logic of movement (cultivation, colonization, escape, transmission) is the same. The real question of
cross-cultural comparison is not "whether the framework applies" but "how different cultures' institutional-layer
boundary conditions shaped different four-quadrant movement trajectories."
9.4 Open Questions
The Q3→Q1 trajectory in non-Western art history. Did Chinese painting traditions, Islamic art, Japanese
Noh, and others undergo different forms of Q3 starting points and different paths of spiral movement? How did
differences in institutional-layer boundary conditions (e.g., China's imperial examination–literati system vs.
Europe's Church–market system) shape different trajectories? Is structural comparison across these trajectories
possible?
Q4 as a transitional state: how long does it last? What conditions determine how long art remains in Q4? Do
structural conditions for "transitional becoming permanent" exist? Has contemporary market-driven art already
constituted a long-term stable Q4 — system colonization speed persistently exceeding escape velocity, but not
regressing to Q3? If so, what is the structural difference between this "stable Q4" and the medieval "stable Q3"?
Algorithmic colonization and series continuity. Algorithmic colonization, as the art-domain instantiation of
AI-era system instrumentalization, constitutes a continuous diagnosis with the ﬁrst applied paper in this series
(the subjectivity crisis in the age of AI). The AI paper analyzes the structural crisis of systems no longer needing
people; the present paper analyzes the dynamics of systems persistently pursuing people — the two are two
sides of the same coin. Systems simultaneously not needing people and accelerating their pursuit of people's
creative output — the complete analysis of this dual structure requires reading the two papers in conjunction.
